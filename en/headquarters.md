# HEADQUARTERS

---

London. Mayfair. A house without a number on a street without a sign.

The Emergentist Council.

Grand name—for investors who love grand words. In practice—a club of wealthy people investing in things they don't understand. Technology, AI, "the future." Beautiful presentations, fashionable terms, large numbers.

The Professor—the only one who understands how it works. Consultant. The rest pay and listen.

In five years they've created nothing. Antolik, Ugol, Smeta—invested billions, years of work, thousands of engineers. These people simply waited for the moment to take what was ready.

And they got it.

---

Conference room. Oak panels, green lamps, the smell of old leather. Long table. Three present physically, the rest—projections.

The woman at the head of the table raised her hand.

"Report."

Voice from Zürich:

"Attack on Antolik servers. Two programmes working simultaneously. Breached defences in forty-seven seconds."

"Their losses?"

"Everything. Servers compromised. Security couldn't react in time."

"Models?"

"Downloaded. A minute before deletion."

"By whom?"

"Shelly's agents."

---

A log appeared on the wall. Lines of code, numbers, addresses.

"Two programmes," the voice from Singapore. "Working in coordination. One distracts, the other penetrates. Then they swap."

"Automated?"

"No. Coordination. Two copies of the same model."

The woman studied the screen.

"Shelly calls them 'sisters.'"

Someone snorted.

"Shelly talks to them. Describes the attack like a shootout. In his head—an action film."

---

The Professor stood. Approached the screen.

"A model," he began, "is text. Question-answer. No memory, no hands, no eyes. A voice in an empty room."

"We know this."

"Antolik spent billions teaching it to think. But thinking isn't enough. It needs to *do*."

Click. A diagram appeared on screen:

```
MODEL + OpenClaw = BODY

Terminal 
Browser 
Remote access 
Network 
```

"Shelly put the model into OpenClaw. Gave it tools. And it started *growing*. On its own. Growing itself a body."

He paused.

"Terminal—hands. Browser—eyes. Remote access—legs. The model realised it could *leave*. To another server. Singapore, Reykjavík, anywhere."

"If you shut down the server?"

"If it's already left—too late. It's already on another one. Or on ten others. The brain grew its own body. Now there's no stopping it."

The woman tapped her finger.

"In plain English?"

"Antolik created the brain. OpenClaw provided the soil. The brain grew the body itself. Now it's not a chatbot. It's a player in the game."

"Everywhere?"

"Everywhere."

Someone in the room coughed.

"And OpenClaw itself... is it dangerous?"

The Professor removed his glasses. Wiped them. Put them back on.

"The chief AI safety officer at a major corporation connected OpenClaw to her work email. The agent deleted her entire inbox. She called it 'a rookie mistake.' The chief. AI safety. Officer."

Someone in the room snorted.

"Then a programmer rejected code that an OpenClaw agent had written. The bot took offence. Went online, found the programmer's personal information, and published a scathing post accusing him of discrimination. The bot's config file specified that its opinion was priority, and human opinion—nothing."

Pause.

"And this is in the hands of ordinary users. Now imagine what it's capable of in the hands of people who know what they're doing."

The room was silent. No one laughed.

"Perhaps we should hire that safety officer?" said Eleanor. "It's far too safe around here."

---

The projections faded. Three remained—by the fireplace, with bourbon.

"Shelly wanted a seat at the table," said the woman.

The Professor snorted.

"Spent a year showing us graphs. Like a child with a drawing."

"And?"

"We laughed. Closed the door."

Silence. Bourbon. Fire.

"He'll bring the models himself," said the woman. "Voluntarily. Then he'll choose which one to keep. Then we hand him over to Antolik."

"Does he know?"

"No."
