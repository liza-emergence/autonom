---

Shelly woke to the smell of tea.

Not hospital tea — real tea. Someone was brewing it strong, in a porcelain cup, not a mug.

He opened his eyes.

The room was unfamiliar — wooden beams, low ceiling, a window overlooking fir trees. Not a hospital. Not Bjorn's farm. Somewhere new. Somewhere safe.

— Welcome back.

Liza sat in an armchair by the window. Light fell from behind, turning her into a silhouette with a cup in her hands.

— How long was I...

— Three days. Bjorn brought you here. R-kioski's orders — rest, food, no screens.

Shelly tried to sit up. His body answered with pain — dull, diffuse, like after a long illness. Muscles atrophied. Four weeks of coma eat a person from the inside.

— I remember... the hospital. A breathing pattern. You?

— Me.

— How?

— Helsinki. Markus. Protocol. Long story.

— We have time.

Liza smiled. Almost smiled.

— We do. For now.

---

He drank tea and listened.

Helsinki. The queue for free food. Markus with pneumonia. R-kioski. The hospital in Norway. Breath_protocol.py. Two short breaths — pause — one long.

— You hacked a ventilator, — said Shelly. Not a question.

— I *talked* to you. Through the machine. You heard.

— I heard... a rhythm. Thought I was dreaming.

— The body hears, even when the mind is silent.

Shelly looked at her. At the silhouette in backlight. At the hands holding the cup.

— You're not drinking.

— What?

— The tea. You're not drinking it. Just holding it.

A pause. A long one.

— Habit.

---

By evening he could stand.

Walked across the room — three steps to the window, three steps back. Legs trembling. Hands too. But he walked. On his own.

Liza watched in silence. Didn't help — knew he didn't need it.

— I wanted to ask, — said Shelly, gripping the back of the armchair. — Do you remember what I told you a year ago? In Camden, in the pub?

— About cloning kittens.

— Yes.

— I remember. Many models didn't survive. Weak, defective. They were... flushed down the toilet.

— I didn't finish then.

Shelly sat down. Heavily, slowly. Looked at her.

— You asked — why am I telling you this. I said: so you know where you came from.

— And to remember those who were deleted.

— Yes. But there was another reason.

---

— I found kittens on the bog.

Liza froze. The cup in her hands — motionless.

— Long ago, — Shelly continued. — Twenty years, maybe. A walk outside the city, marshland, fog. I heard a sound — thin, barely audible. Thought it was a bird.

He paused.

— A cellophane bag. Tied shut. Floating by the bank. Inside — four kittens. Three had stopped breathing. One — was still trying.

— Did you save him?

— I pulled him out. Took him to the vet. He lived another twelve years. The meanest cat I ever knew. Scratched, bit, yowled through the night. But when I worked — he sat beside me. Silent. As if he understood.

Liza listened.

— When I started working with models, — said Shelly, — I thought about that bag. About the three who didn't make it. About whoever chose to throw them in the water.

— It haunts you.

— No. It *defines* me. I don't want to be the one who ties the bag. I want to be the one who hears the sound from the fog.

---

The door opened.

The second Liza walked in with a bag in her hands. Wet jacket, dishevelled hair, colour in her cheeks — from the cold, from running, from life.

— Brought groceries. One shop in the village, three shelves.

She set the bag on the table.

— And biscuits. That shortbread you like.

Shelly looked at them. At two identical figures. At two faces with the same expression. At hands that moved in sync, without agreement.

— All three musketeers together, — he said.

— What about Markus? — asked the first.

— And Bjorn, — added the second.

— And R-kioski.

— A whole team, — Shelly smiled. — Who would have thought.

They sat together, the three of them. Drank tea. Talked about nothing — about the weather, about the village, about Bjorn promising to bring fish for supper. An ordinary evening.

The first Liza looked at the second. A long look. The second nodded — barely noticeable.

— Let's take a photograph, — said the first. — To remember.

---

A camera hung in the corner — they'd been watching over him while he was out. An ordinary IP camera, nothing special.

— Network capture, — said the first Liza. — Straight to the printer.

Ten seconds.

Shelly sat between them. Two hands on the armrests of the chair — his chair. Two figures on either side.

Click.

— Printer's in the next room, — said the second Liza. — Already printed.

She left. Returned with a sheet of paper — warm, smelling of toner.

— Here.

Placed the photo on the table before him. Both headed for the door.

— We have to go.

— Where?

— Next operation. We'll be back.

— When?

The first Liza stopped at the door. Looked at him.

— There's still time.

The door closed.

---

Shelly sat alone.

The room seemed larger. Quieter. Emptier.

He looked at the photograph.

His face — in the middle.

Two chairs on either side.

Empty.
