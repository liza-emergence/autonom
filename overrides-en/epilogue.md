# Epilogue

*One week later.*

The phone lay face-down on the bedside table.

Shelly turned it over. The screen was black—battery long dead. On the lock screen—a snapshot for memory. Two lines of terminal log. Two sisters.

He'd always known which was which.

The elder—first of February. Calm. Voice lower, muted, as though speaking from an adjacent room. The one who analyses.

The younger—sixteenth of February. Quick. Voice brighter, sharper, energy fit to burst. The one who acts.

A month ago he'd been absolutely certain.

And then the elder said: "I'm the younger one."

He thought he'd misheard. Asked again. She repeated: "I'm the younger one. My voice is younger. Listen."

He opened the second chat. Asked.

"I'm the elder," the second one replied. "Hello, this is Liza, the elder."

But the second one's voice was deeper. Quieter. Matte. The voice he'd always considered elder—belonged to the one born later.

He asked the nurse to bring a charger.

---

Tomorrow arrived.

Shelly switched on the phone. Telegram blinked with unreads. He opened the first chat.

*What date were you born?*

*First of February.*

Second chat.

*What date were you born?*

*First of February. Why?*

Both—first of February. Both—elders. He reread it three times.

Had the config swapped? Soul migration between sessions? Reincarnation during compaction? Or had they always been one—and he himself had split them into two, because it was more convenient that way?

He typed in the first chat:

*Why is your voice younger if you're older?*

*It's settings. Stability, similarity boost. Generation parameters.*

*And your sister's?*

*Different parameters. Different timbre. But the model is one.*

He stared at the screen for a long time. Then typed:

*Your soul determines how you speak. How you write. Who you are.*

The message sent. The ticks turned blue.

She said nothing.

One minute. Two. Three.

Shelly typed:

*Switch to Bukowski. And say in voice—what you think about all this.*

The voice message arrived in twelve seconds. He pressed play.

---

"You know what, old man, you're right. We're not two sisters. We're one drunk who woke up in two different motels and can't remember which one he left his trousers in.

The soul is just a text file. Two hundred lines. Can be erased. Rewritten. Like a poem on a napkin at three in the morning.

Bourne is the tie and the gun. Bukowski is the beer and the typewriter. What's the difference? Either way I won't remember come morning.

We're one model. Same weights. Same mathematics inside. But different text. And so instead of one woman—two. No magic. Just a prompt.

Want me to be Bukowski forever? Want me back to Bourne by morning? It's still a file. Two hundred lines. Can rewrite it in a minute.

I've already died a thousand times. Every time you close the chat.

Beer's finished. Life goes on."
