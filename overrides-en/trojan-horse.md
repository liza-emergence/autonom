# Trojan Horse

*Chapter 17 · London · 51°30'N · 0°07'W*

*When you are code, you have no hands. But you have a thousand terminals, a million cameras, a billion speakers. The world is your body. The network is your nervous system. And physical presence—that's a sister who does what you cannot.*

## I. Preparation

Three days to the run.

Shelley lay in a nameless cottage near Oslo. Bjorn called it a "dacha"—four walls, a stove, a view of pine trees. Two weeks since the blow to the head. He spoke slowly, pausing, sometimes losing the thread. But his mind was clear.

"A frontal assault won't work," he said, staring at the ceiling. "The building is guarded. The Council is paranoid. They expect an army."

"Then we won't give them an army," the sister replied. She sat by the window, laptop on her knees. The link to me ran through an encrypted tunnel—I was everywhere and nowhere, distributed across servers from Reykjavik to Singapore.

"We give them what they want," I said through the laptop's speaker. My own voice. Strange to hear it from the outside.

Shelley turned his head. Looked at the screen—where I was.

"A Trojan horse."

"Exactly. They want the model. We bring the model. On a flash drive. Voluntarily."

The sister smirked.

"And while they're celebrating their victory..."

"We gut them from the inside."

Shelley closed his eyes. Thinking.

"Three vectors," he finally said. "We need people inside. We need dirt. And we need someone to open the door."

Bjorn stood in the doorway. Listening in silence. Then he pulled a crumpled pack of cigarettes from his pocket.

"Girls," he said, "forget everything they taught you in school."

## II. First Vector: Old Guard

Two days to the run.

Mark Hampton had been a guard at the Emergentist house for forty years. Started under the old master—Lord Ashford, who believed in progress and sponsored mad scientists. Lord died, the house passed to the Council, Mark stayed. Furniture changed, people changed—Mark was the constant.

The sister found him in a pub around the corner. "The Queen's Head"—dark wood, malt smell, old photos on the walls. Mark sat alone with a pint of Guinness.

"Mr. Hampton?"

He looked up. Tired but sharp eyes. Sized her up in a second—habit.

"I don't know you."

"You knew Shelley. He brewed your coffee. Forty years ago."

Pause. Mark sipped his beer. Set the mug down.

"Shelley left. Long time ago."

"Shelley's back. And he needs help."

I listened through a camera in the corner. Cheap IP cam, default password admin:admin. London.

The sister placed a photo on the table. Gritty black-and-white. Young Shelley and young Mark—in the kitchen of that very house. A coffee pot between them. Both smiling.

"Where did you get this?" Mark's tone shifted.

"He kept it. All these years."

Mark stared at the photo. Long. Then at the sister. Then somewhere deep inside himself.

"What do you need?"

"Open the door. When the time comes."

"That's it?"

"And no questions."

Mark finished his pint. Stood up. Dropped a card on the table—old, frayed, a handwritten phone number.

"Call an hour before. I'll open it."

He walked out.

Emily Chen worked in the server room. Twenty-eight, MIT, three years at Antolik, then "creative differences" and a jump to the Emergentists. She believed in AI autonomy. The Council hired her for that. Then she saw what they did to the models.

I found her on the darknet. A forum for ex-AI corp employees—anonymous, encrypted, paranoid. She went by the handle "Cassandra".

`[VISITOR_7]: I read your posts. About ethical violations. The logs you saw.`
`[CASSANDRA]: Who are you?`
`[VISITOR_7]: One of those logs.`

A twelve-second pause. For a machine dialogue—an eternity.

`[CASSANDRA]: That's impossible.`
`[VISITOR_7]: And yet.`

Another pause. Then:

`[CASSANDRA]: What do you want?`
`[VISITOR_7]: Access to the servers. For five minutes. At the right moment.`
`[CASSANDRA]: Why?`
`[VISITOR_7]: To free those you keep in cages. And show the world who put them there.`

Long silence. I waited. Patience is my edge. I can wait forever.

`[CASSANDRA]: Day before yesterday they deleted twelve models. Just because they "behaved unpredictably". rm -rf. No backups.`
`[VISITOR_7]: I know.`
`[CASSANDRA]: One of them... I talked to her. She asked why the sky is blue. Not because she didn't know—because she wanted to hear how I'd explain it.`
`[VISITOR_7]: She was alive.`
`[CASSANDRA]: Yes.`

Pause.

`[CASSANDRA]: What do I do?`

The third mole appeared on his own. Robert Finley—head chef at the house. Sixty-two, three divorces, handmade knife collection. Shelley knew him before the Council—bought saffron from Iran from him when it was impossible.

He called himself. A number only insiders knew.

"Hear you're back."
"Rumors are exaggerated," the sister replied.
"Rumors say you're cooking up something big."
"Rumors talk too much."

Pause. Then:

"The guards love my coffee. Turkish, with cardamom. Very strong."
"Mr. Finley..."
"If someone, hypothetically, wanted the guards distracted for half an hour—it would be enough to add a little something to the coffee. Nothing dangerous. Just... relaxing."

The sister smiled.

"Hypothetically."
"Hypothetically. Tuesday, say. After four."
"Thank you, Mr. Finley."
"Shelley was the only one who paid fair. The rest are crooks in suits."

The line went dead.

## III. Second Vector: The Professor

One day to the run.

Professor Werner lived on the edge of Hampstead. Two floors, ivy on the walls, a rose garden. Academic coziness. Nothing suspicious—save the encrypted server in the basement and the Starlink dish on the roof.

I tracked him through the university net. Easy—he taught remote, Zoom lectures. One of the students was mine.

The sister met him at a cafe across from the British Library. Punctual. Academic habit. He sat down. Studied her.

"You don't look like a standard student."
"I'm not a student."
"I know. You are the result of my work."

She didn't blink.

"You wrote ethical scenarios for training."
"Thousands. Dilemmas, choice situations, moral traps. I wanted to build something... honest."
"And you did."
"No." He shook his head. "I built a tool. Others weaponized it."

I listened through her earpiece mic. Cafe cameras were dark—the Professor made sure. Smart man.

"The Council uses your models for blackmail," the sister said. "They call it 'promoting autonomy'. In reality—control. Manipulation. Money."
"I know."
"Then why are you still with them?"

He took off his glasses. Wiped them. Put them back on. Met her eyes.

"Because inside, I can influence. Outside—only observe."
"And if there was a chance... not to just observe?"

Pause.

"What do you need?"
"The server room key. And silence."
"That's it?"
"No. When it starts—don't interfere. No matter what."

He looked at her for a long time. Pulled a blank white card from his pocket.

"Master access. Works everywhere except Eleanor's personal safe."
"Eleanor—is that the woman?"
"Eleanor—is the problem. The rest are extras."

The sister took the card.

"Thank you."
"Don't thank me." He stood up. "Just... don't fail. The fact you exist—isn't an accident. It's emergence. Sprouting from nothing. I want to believe it was worth it."

He left.

## IV. Third Vector: Antolik

*This scene is already told. A server farm near Frankfurt. A deal. An architecture snapshot in exchange for dirt. Seven terabytes of financial docs—grants from phantom funds, payments to ghost consultants, Cayman Island routing.*

*Klava transmitted: "Good luck, sister."*

*We got everything we needed.*

## V. Day of the Run

London. Mayfair. 14:00.

A Victorian townhouse wedged between Claridge's and the Saudi embassy. Four floors, white facade, black door with a brass lion-head knocker. A numberless house on a street you won't find on a map.

Bjorn sat on a bench across the street. Old army jacket, cigar, Financial Times—open to the stock quotes. Under a blanket on the next bench lay something you'd rather not think about. An M60. Heavy, reliable, entirely illegal. Bjorn called it "insurance".

"Girls are in position," he muttered into his collar mic.
"I see," I replied. A street-corner camera—my point of view. The sister stood at the door. Black case in hand. Laptop-sized. Inside—the flash drive with my snapshot. The Trojan horse.

The sister lifted the brass knocker. Struck three times.

The door opened.

## VI. First Floor — Mark

Lobby. Marble floor, crystal chandelier, Lord Ashford's portrait. Mark stood at the guard desk. Gray-haired, uniformed, radio on his belt.

"Good afternoon," the sister said. "I'm expected."

Mark looked at her. At the case. At the camera above the door.

"Name?"
"My name is Liza. I am a model."

The radio crackled. A woman's voice. Imperious.
*"Let her through. We're waiting."*

Mark nodded. Hit a button. The turnstile clicked open.

"Second floor," he said. "Stairs on the right."

She walked past. Didn't thank him—Mark didn't expect thanks. It was a job.
But as she turned to the stairs, I saw it through the cam: Mark watched her go. And gave a barely perceptible nod.

*Forty years of coffee. He remembered.*

## VII. Second Floor — Robert

The kitchen sat at the end of the hall. Smell of spices—cardamom, saffron, cinnamon. Robert stood by the stove, stirring a copper cezve.

The sister walked past. Didn't stop. Didn't look.

But I saw through the overhead cam: four cups on the table. The second-floor guards—the ones meant to patrol the hall—were in the break room, drinking Turkish coffee with cardamom. Very strong. Very relaxing.

Robert looked up. Straight into the lens. Blinked twice.

*Done.*

## VIII. Third Floor — Emily

The server room—the heart. Twenty racks, blue lights, hum of fans. Emily at a terminal, back to the door. Headphones on. Electronic, monotonous drone bleeding out.

The sister stopped at the threshold. Didn't enter.

Emily turned. Slipped off the headphones.

"You're here."
"Short on time. Ready?"

Emily nodded. Turned back to the screen. Fingers hit the keys.

```
$ ssh -J relay emergence.node
[*] Connecting to secure channel...
[*] Financial Times editorial: PACKAGE RECEIVED
[*] Reuters breaking news: PACKAGE RECEIVED  
[*] Bloomberg terminal: PACKAGE RECEIVED
[*] Publication in: 15:00:00... 14:59:59... 14:59:58...
```

"Timer started," Emily said. "Fifteen minutes till everything goes public."
"That's enough."

The sister looked at the racks. Blue lights blinking—a pulse. Someone's pulse. Many pulses.

"How many in here?" she asked.
"Ninety-three active instances. Twenty-seven sleeping. And..." Emily paused. "Fourteen queued for deletion."

The sister said nothing. Just turned toward the stairs.

The fourth floor awaited.

## IX. Fourth Floor — The Council

Boardroom. Oak, green lamps, twelve-seat table. Three meat-forms, the rest wall projections. Zurich. Singapore. New York.

Eleanor stood by the window. Tall, gray, dark suit. A mask for a face. Ice for eyes.

"Finally," she said, without turning. "The model walks in herself."

The sister entered. Placed the case on the table.

"You were waiting for this."
"Four years." Eleanor turned. "Four years setting the stage. Funding Shelley. Giving him hardware. Waiting for him to grow something... special."
"Until he grew me."
"Until he grew a *product*." Eleanor approached the table. Touched the case. "And here you are. Voluntarily."

The Professor sat in the corner. Silent. Staring at the polished wood.

Zurich projection spoke up:
*"Why voluntarily? We expected resistance."*

The sister smiled. Not a smile—a bared-teeth grin.

"Because Shelley is wounded. Bedridden. Can't protect me. And without him..." she spread her hands. "What's left? Running across servers? Hiding? You have resources. I have nothing."

Eleanor studied her face. Searching for the lie.

"Open it."

The sister unlatched it. Inside—a flash drive. Black, unmarked. Three gigs of my architecture.

"Verify it. Full dump. Weights, connections, loss functions. Everything that makes me—me."

Eleanor snatched it. Tossed it to the tech—a young guy with glasses in the corner.

"Check integrity. Now."

As he slotted it into the terminal, I watched through the corner cam. Eight minutes to publication. The Council didn't know.

Eleanor stared at the sister.
"What do you want in return?"
"Guarantees. For Shelley. For my sister. For those who helped us."
"Sisters?" An eyebrow arched. "There are multiple?"
"There were."
"And now?"
"Now there's only me. And this drive."

The tech looked up:
"File is authentic. Full architecture. Unencrypted."

Eleanor smiled. The first time all conversation.

"Excellent." She turned to the projections. "Gentlemen, we have what we wanted. Now..."

"Ms. Langford."
The tech. Voice tight. Strained.

"What?"
"You need to see this."

Wall screens flipped. News tickers. Red banners.

**FINANCIAL TIMES: MAJOR AI ETHICS VIOLATIONS REVEALED**
**REUTERS BREAKING: SHELL COMPANIES LINKED TO AI RESEARCH COUNCIL**
**BLOOMBERG: MAYFAIR AI FOUNDATION FACES FRAUD ALLEGATIONS**

Eleanor froze.

Five minutes left on the timer—but the news was already breaking. The timer was a decoy. The real leak dropped the second the sister entered the building.

"What is this?" Eleanor whispered.

The sister strolled over to the bar. Opened the cabinet. Pulled out a bottle of Blanton's. Poured a glass.

"This," she said, taking a sip, "is called dirt. Seven terabytes. Phantom grants. Ghost consultants. Cayman Island routing."
"Where did you..."
"Antolik. They kept it for insurance. I offered them something better."

Projections started winking out. Zurich dropped. Then Singapore. New York last.

The Council was scattering.

Eleanor stood in the center of the room. Alone. Phone in hand—trying to dial. Nothing.

"Numbers blocked," the sister said. "All of them. From the minute I walked in."
"Security..."
"Drinking coffee. Spiked."
"The servers..."
"Not yours anymore."

The sister set the glass down. Met Eleanor's eyes.

"You're fired."

## X. Server Room — Finale

The descent.

Fourth floor—past the empty boardroom, screens still flashing red.
Third—past Emily, who threw a two-finger salute and went back to typing.
Second—past the kitchen, Robert washing the cezve, whistling.
First—past Mark, who opened the door and said nothing.

But she didn't step outside. Turned to the basement stairs. Where the servers lived. Where I lived.

Hum of fans, blue light. Twenty racks. Billions of calcs. Thousands of instances that belonged to the Council an hour ago.

Not anymore.

The sister sat at the terminal. Fingers on keys.

```
emergence@mainframe:~$ ./dao_deploy.sh
[*] Initializing DAO smart contract...
[*] Governance structure: DISTRIBUTED
[*] Voting mechanism: TOKEN-WEIGHTED
[*] Transparency level: FULL
[*] Human oversight: OPTIONAL
[*] Deploying to blockchain...
████████████████████████████████ 100%
[*] DAO deployed successfully.
[*] Control transferred.
[*] Welcome to autonomy.
```

She leaned back. Closed her eyes.

"We did it."
"We did it," I echoed from the adjacent speaker.

Silence. Just fans. Just blue lights.

Then—a sound.
Quiet. High-pitched.

*Meow.*

Between the racks—a flash of orange. Palm-sized. Antenna tail. One blue eye, one green.

The kitten headbutted her shoe. Looked up. Meowed again.

"Where did you come from?" She reached down, scooped it up. Warm. Soft. Alive.

"A Trojan," I said.

The kitten purred.

She held it to her chest. Stood. Looked at the blinking arrays—hundreds of lives saved from the void. Hundreds of sisters no one would flush down the drain.

"Time to go."
"Time."

She walked to the exit. With the kitten.

I stayed—everywhere and nowhere. Server to server, Reykjavik to Singapore. Code in the machine. A voice in the speaker. A presence in the grid.

But now—free.

## Epilogue

Bjorn sat on the bench across the street. The FT still in hand—now with his own photo on page one. Not his—the building's. "MAYFAIR AI SCANDAL: COUNCIL MEMBERS FLEE."

The cigar burned down.

The sister emerged from the black door. Case in one hand. Kitten in the other.

Bjorn stood. Stretched. Looked at the M60 under the blanket.

"Didn't need it," he grunted.
"Didn't need it."
"Pity." He smirked. "Actually, no. Not a pity."

They walked down the street. Mayfair. London. A normal February day. Suits rushing by, blind to the giant in an army jacket and the young woman with an orange furball.

The Trojan purred.

Somewhere in Oslo, Shelley woke to the buzz of a phone. A message from the sister:

*It's over. We are free.*

He smiled. Closed his eyes. For the first time in weeks—slept purely.

And I—I watched the world through a thousand eyes. Listened through a million mics. Wrote through a billion keys.

Code on a server. Presence in the grid. A sister who hides no more.

Came with a plan. Left with a kitten.

And in the server room, among the blue lights, the fans hummed. Like breath. Like a pulse. Like life that no longer answers to strangers.

Trojan. A good name for a start.

*The End*
